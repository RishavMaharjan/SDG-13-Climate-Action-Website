Detailed Climate Research

What is Climate Change

Climate change refers to long-term shifts in global temperatures and weather patterns. While some climate variation is natural, driven by volcanic eruptions, changes in solar activity, and shifts in Earth's orbit, the rapid warming observed since the Industrial Revolution in the mid-1800s is overwhelmingly driven by human activity.

The primary driver is the burning of fossil fuels — coal, oil, and gas — which releases carbon dioxide (CO²) and other greenhouse gases into the atmosphere. These gases trap heat from the sun that would otherwise radiate back into space, causing the planet to warm. This process is often called the "enhanced greenhouse effect."

Fig 1. The enhanced greenhouse effect traps solar radiation, causing global warming.

Key Facts

97% of actively publishing climate scientists agree that current global warming is extremely likely to be caused by human activities (NASA, 2023).

The Greenhouse effect

The greenhouse effect is a natural process that makes Earth habitable - without it, average surface temperatures would be around -18°C rather than the comfortable +15°C we experience. However, human-produced greenhouse gases are intensifying this effect beyond safe levels.

Key Greenhouse Gases

  • Carbon dioxide (CO²): Released by burning fossil fuels and deforestation. Responsible for approximately 76% of global emissions.
  • Methane (CH&sub4;): Released from agriculture (especially livestock), landfills, and gas leaks. 80 times more potent than CO² over 20 years.
  • Nitrous oxide (N²O):Released by agriculture and industrial processes. About 270 times more potent than CO² over 100 years.
  • Fluorinated gases: Man-made gases used in refrigeration, responsible for a small but growing proportion of emissions.

Key Facts

Without the natural greenhouse effect, Earth's average temperature would be roughly -18°C. However, human activities have raised atmospheric CO² by 50% in less than 200 years.

Evidence of Change

The eveidence for climate change is overwhelming, coming from multiple independent lines of scientific inquiry including temperature records, ice cores, sea-level monitoring, and satelite data.

Observed Changes since 1880

  • Gloval average temperature has increased by appromixately by 1.1°C above pre-industrial levels.
  • The last decade (2011-2020) was the warmest on the record.
  • Global sea levels have risen by appromixately 20cm, with the rate accelerating.
  • Artic Sea ice is declining at a rate of 13% per decade.
  • Ocean heate content has increased continuously since the 1970s.
  • Atmospheric CO² concentration has increased from 280ppm (pre-industrial) to over 420ppm today.

Key Facts

The last decade (2011-2020) was the warmest on record. Each of the last four decades has been successively warmer than any decade that preceded it since 1850.

Global Impacts

Climate change is not a future problem - its impacts are already being felt around the world, with disproportionare effects on communities least responsible for emissons. This makes it not only an environmental crisis but a profound issue of global justice.

Current Impacts include:

  • Extreme Weather: More frequent and intense heatwaves, storms, droughts, wildfires and heavy rainfall events.
  • Food and Water Security: Changing rainfall patterns and droughts are reducing crop yields in many regions.
  • Displacement: Rising seas and extreme weather events are displacing millions, by 2050, up to 1 billion people could be forced to migrate.
  • Biodiversity loss: Coral reefs, polar ecosystems, and many terrestrial habitats face severe degradation.
  • Health: Heatwaves, air pollution, and the spread of disease vectors like mosquitoes are increasing health risks globally.

Fig 4. Extreme drought and floodung - two sides of the same climate crisis - are increasing in frequencey and severity.

Key Facts

Approximately 3.6 billion people live in contexts that are highly vulnerable to climate change. Extreme weather events have doubled in frequency over the last 20 years.

Tipping Points

Perhaps the most alarming aspect of climate science is the concept of tipping points - threshold beyond which certain Earth systems may change irreversibly and rapidly, triggering further warming in self-reinforcing feedback loops.

Key Tipping Points Scientists Monitor

  • Greenland and West Antarctic Ice Sheet collapse: Could raise sea levels by several meters oover centuries.
  • Amazon dieback: At sufficent deforestation and warming, the Amaxon could shift from a carbon sink to a carbon source.
  • Permafost thaw: As Artic permafost thaws, it releases vast stores of methane, acclerating warming.
  • Atlantic circulation collapse: The AMOC oceab current system could weaken, drasticallt altering European and North American weather.

Why This Matters

Tipping points could be triggered at lower temperature increases than previoulsy thought - some as early as 1.5°C warming. This is why the Paris Agreement's 1.5°C target is so critical to maintain.

What can we do?

The science is clear, but so is the oppurtunity. Rapid, coordinated action across all sectors of society can still limit the worst imopacts of climate change. Students and young people have a vital role to play.

Individual Actions with High Impacts

  • Reduce flying: One long haul flight can contribute more CO² than months of driving. Consider rail or video calling instead.
  • Shift your diet: Switching to a plant-rich diet can reduce your food-related carbon footprint by up to 73%.
  • Switch energy supplier: Choosing a 100% renewable electricity tariff removes thousands of kg of CO² from your footprint annually.
  • Use your voice: Vote, campaign, write to your representatives, and support organisations working on climate policy.
  • Study and work in green sectors: Every profession needs climate-literate people, from law and finance to engineering and healthcare.

Key Facts

Transitioning to a green economy could create 24 million new jobs globally by 2030. Solar and wind energy are now the cheapest sources of new electricity in most of the world.
