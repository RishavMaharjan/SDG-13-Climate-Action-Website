Renewable Energy Content

Overview

Renewable energy comes from sources that are naturally replenished — sunlight, wind, water and geothermal heat. Unlike fossil fuels, these sources produce little to no greenhouse gas emissions, making them central to addressing climate change under SDG 13.

The burning of coal, oil and gas accounts for roughly 75% of global greenhouse gas emissions. Transitioning to renewables is the single largest lever available to keep warming below 1.5°C as set out in the Paris Agreement.

Fig 1. A utility-scale solar farm — clean energy infrastructure expanding worldwide.

Key Fact

Renewables accounted for 30% of global electricity in 2023, with solar PV costs falling 89% since 2010 (IRENA, 2023).

Solar Power

Photovoltaic (PV) panels convert sunlight directly into electricity through the photoelectric effect. They are scalable, silent and produce zero emissions during operation.

Advantages and Challenges

  • Zero operational emissions — no air or noise pollution during use.
  • Rapidly falling costs — now below $0.05/kWh in many regions.
  • Intermittency — only generates during daylight; requires battery storage.
  • Land use — large-scale farms require significant space.

Fig 2. Rooftop solar PV allows homes and businesses to generate their own clean electricity.

Key Fact

Solar PV is now the cheapest source of new electricity generation in history, making it a cornerstone of the climate solution (IEA, 2023).

Wind Energy

Wind turbines convert the kinetic energy of moving air into electricity. With lifecycle emissions of just 7–15 gCO²/kWh versus 820 gCO²/kWh for coal, wind is one of the cleanest energy technologies available.

Onshore vs. Offshore

  • Onshore wind is the cheapest form of new electricity in most countries, with minimal land disruption — farmland between turbines remains usable.
  • Offshore wind captures stronger, more consistent winds but is costlier to install. The UK, Denmark and China are global leaders.

Fig 3. Onshore wind turbines — farmland around the turbines can still be used for crops and grazing.

Key Fact

Global wind capacity exceeded 900 GW in 2023, covering approximately 7% of global electricity demand (IRENA, 2023).

Hydropower

Hydropower is the world's largest renewable electricity source, supplying around 16% of global electricity. It works by converting the potential energy of water flowing through a dam or river channel into electricity via turbines.

Fig 4. A hydroelectric dam and reservoir — the most established form of renewable electricity generation.

Types of Hydropower

  • Large-scale dams — high capacity but significant ecological and community impact.
  • Run-of-river — diverts river flow with minimal storage and less disruption.
  • Pumped-storage — acts as a giant battery, storing surplus energy for peak demand.
  • Small-scale (<10 MW) — ideal for remote communities; Nepal has enormous untapped potential.

Key Fact

Nepal has an estimated hydropower potential of 83,000 MW but only around 2,000 MW is currently developed — a major opportunity for clean regional energy.

Geothermal Energy

Geothermal energy is the heat from within the Earth. It can be captured from hot water and steam beneath the surface to provide clean, consistent electricity, known as base load power. Unlike solar and wind, it does not depend on weather conditions.

Fig 5. A geothermal power plant — providing continuous base load power from the Earth's interior heat.

Key Fact

Iceland generates over 30% of its electricity from geothermal sources, proving its potential as a massive fossil-fuel-free energy provider in volcanic regions.

What Students Can Do

Individual and collective action by students can meaningfully support the shift to renewable energy and contribute to the goals of SDG 13.

High-Impact Actions

  • Switch to a green energy tariff — choose a supplier sourcing 100% renewable electricity.
  • Reduce energy use — turn off standby devices and enable power-saving modes.
  • Advocate for campus solar — push your university to install rooftop PV panels.
  • Use public transport or cycle — transport is the hardest sector to decarbonise.
  • Study green subjects — every profession needs climate-literate people.

Key Fact

Transitioning to a green economy could create 24 million new jobs globally by 2030. Clean energy is no longer a trade-off with affordability (IEA, 2023).
